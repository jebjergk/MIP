What is MIP?

MIP stands for Market Intelligence Platform. It is a system that watches financial markets every day, looks for patterns in how prices are moving, and tests whether acting on those patterns would have made money — all without spending a single real dollar.

Think of it this way

MIP is a flight simulator for investing. Just as a pilot practices flying in a simulator before stepping into a real cockpit, MIP lets you practice and observe investment strategies in a safe, simulated environment. No real money is at risk. Ever.

What Is a Price Bar?

Every trading day, each asset produces a single price bar — a compact summary of everything that happened to its price during that day.

Scenario: Apple's Trading Day

Imagine watching Apple stock on January 15, 2026:

  • 2:15 PM — Broad market dip pulls Apple down to $190.25 (the day's Low)
  • 4:00 PM — Market closes. Apple's last trade is at $195.80 (the Close)
  • Throughout the day, 42 million shares changed hands (the Volume)

Together, these are called an OHLCV bar. MIP stores one bar per asset per day.

What Is a "Return"?

A return is simply how much an asset's price changed, expressed as a percentage.

Scenario: Calculating a Return

Apple closed at $192.00 on Monday and $195.80 on Tuesday.

Return = ($195.80 − $192.00) / $192.00 = +1.98%

Apple went up almost 2% in one day. That's a positive return.

If Apple had closed at $188.00 instead:

Return = ($188.00 − $192.00) / $192.00 = −2.08%

That's a negative return — the price went down.

Key Concept

MIP doesn't care about raw prices. It cares about returns — how much prices are changing. A $200 stock that goes up 1% and a $20 stock that goes up 1% are equally interesting to MIP.

Chapter 4

Patterns and Signals — How MIP Spots Opportunities

What Is a Pattern?

A pattern is a set of rules that examines recent price behavior and decides: "Something noteworthy is happening with this asset right now."

Analogy: Weather Forecasting

A meteorologist might say: "If barometric pressure drops rapidly, humidity rises above 80%, and wind shifts from the southwest, then rain is likely within 24 hours."

That's a pattern — a combination of observable conditions that historically precedes a specific outcome.

MIP works the same way, but with price data instead of weather data, and "continued price increase" instead of rain.

The Momentum Pattern: MIP's Primary Strategy

Core Idea Behind Momentum

"Assets that have been going up recently tend to keep going up for a little while longer."

This is one of the most well-studied phenomena in finance. It doesn't always work, but historically it works often enough to be useful.

Here's how MIP's momentum pattern evaluates an asset, step by step:

Momentum Pattern: Decision Steps
Step 1: Look at returns over the Fast Window (e.g., 3 days) and Slow Window (e.g., 20 days)
▼
Step 2: Is the recent return above the minimum threshold? (e.g., ≥ 0.2%)
▼
Step 3: Has the price been going up consistently, not just one lucky day?
▼
Step 4: Is the current price at or near its recent high?
▼
Step 5: Is this move unusually strong for this asset? (Z-score check)
▼
All checks pass? → SIGNAL GENERATED!

MIP is evaluating Apple (AAPL) on February 19, 2026. Here's what the data shows:

What Is a Signal?

A signal (also called a recommendation) is a record that says:

Signal = A Detection, Not an Action

"Pattern X detected an opportunity in asset Y at time Z, with strength score S."

Signals are not trades. They are observations. Many signals are generated, but only a fraction ever lead to paper trades (after passing trust and risk checks).

Understanding the Score

Each signal has a score that indicates its strength. For momentum signals, the score is typically the asset's recent return.

Hit Rate: The Batting Average

Analogy: Baseball Batting Average

In baseball, a batting average tells you how often a batter gets a hit. A .300 average means they succeed about 30% of the time — and that's considered excellent.

MIP's hit rate works the same way. If a pattern's hit rate is 58%, it means 58 out of every 100 signals ended up making money. MIP requires at least 55%.

Strict Lookahead: No Cheating

Critical Rule

When evaluating a signal, MIP only uses data from after the signal. It never peeks at data from the same time or earlier. This prevents "lookahead bias" — where a system appears to predict the future but is really just using information it already had.

Chapter 6

Trust — How MIP Decides What to Act On

Analogy: Two Weather Forecasters

Forecaster A has been working for 10 years, made thousands of predictions, and is right 62% of the time.

Forecaster B just started last week, made 4 predictions, and was right on all 4.

Which one do you trust more? Most people would say Forecaster A. Four predictions could easily be luck. MIP faces the same dilemma and solves it with a structured trust system.

Three Trust Levels

TRUSTED

Proven track record. Enough data, high hit rate, positive returns.

Action: MIP uses these signals for trading.

WATCH

Shows promise but doesn't yet meet all criteria.

Action: MIP monitors but doesn't trade on them.

UNTRUSTED

Not reliable enough, or insufficient data to judge.

Action: Ignored for trading purposes.

The Training Gate: What It Takes to Be Trusted

RequirementThresholdWhy
Enough signals evaluated≥ 40Need a meaningful sample size. A few lucky wins aren't enough.
Hit rate≥ 55%Must be right more often than wrong.
Average return≥ 0.05%On average, following the signal must produce a positive result.

Why Trust Matters

Trust is the gatekeeper between "MIP noticed something" and "MIP acts on it." Only TRUSTED signals feed into portfolio decisions, trade proposals, and morning briefs. This prevents MIP from betting (even virtual money) on unproven patterns.

Understanding Drawdown

Scenario: How Drawdown Works

Your portfolio starts at $100,000 and grows to $120,000 over a few weeks. That $120,000 is your peak.

Then the market dips and your portfolio drops to $108,000.

Drawdown = ($120,000 − $108,000) / $120,000 = 10%

Notice: you're still up $8,000 from the start! But drawdown measures the drop from the highest point, not the starting point. It captures the pain of watching gains evaporate.

If your profile has a 10% drawdown stop, MIP will now stop making new purchases. It can still sell existing positions, but no new buying until conditions improve.

Chapter 10

The Early-Exit Layer and Decision Console

The Problem: Catching the Fish, Then Throwing It Back

Real-World Analogy

Imagine you go fishing and catch a beautiful trout. You put it in your bucket. Then, because your plan says "fish for four more hours," you keep the line in the water. A wave rolls by, tips the bucket, and the trout flops back into the lake. You had the win and lost it because you followed the plan too rigidly.

This is giveback risk in trading. MIP's daily positions have a planned holding period — say, 5 bars (about a week). But sometimes, the position achieves its expected return within hours. If MIP holds mechanically until the planned exit, the market can reverse and erase those gains. The early-exit layer watches your open positions in real time using 15-minute bars and can recognize when it is time to take the win and walk away.

The Two-Stage Policy

The early-exit system does not simply grab profits the instant a target is reached. That would be like leaving a party the moment you start having fun — you'd miss the best moments. Instead, it uses a two-stage decision:

Stage A — Payoff Detection

Every time new 15-minute bars arrive, MIP checks each open daily position: What is the current return since entry? Has it reached or exceeded the target? If yes, the position becomes an early-exit candidate. The flag goes up, but no whistle blows yet.

Stage B — Giveback Confirmation

Being a candidate does not trigger an exit. MIP now watches for evidence of reversal:

  • Giveback from peak: Has the position given back 40% or more of its best return? Imagine climbing 100 meters up a hill, then sliding 40 meters back down.
  • No new highs: Have the last 3 consecutive 15-minute bars all failed to set a new high? The climb has stalled.
  • Quick payoff rule: If the target was hit within 60 minutes, a stricter threshold (25% giveback) applies, because very fast moves are more likely to snap back.

Only when both stages confirm does MIP signal an early exit.

Why Not Just Exit When Target Is Hit?

Real-World Analogy

You are driving to a destination 100 miles away. After 80 miles, you see a sign saying "Your destination is near." Would you immediately pull over and walk the rest? Of course not — you might be on a highway and the remaining 20 miles could take 15 minutes.

Many of MIP's best trades are continuation moves where hitting the initial target is just the first act. Exiting at the minimum target would systematically cut winners short. The two-stage approach lets winners run when momentum continues, but steps in when the move is clearly fading.

The Safety Ladder: Three Execution Modes

Real-World Analogy

Think of it like a pilot qualifying in a new aircraft: first you fly the simulator, then supervised solo flights, then full certification.

Mode What Happens When to Use
SHADOW Evaluates and logs what would happen, but changes nothing First 2–4 weeks (prove it works)

The system starts in Shadow mode by default. Upgrading requires a deliberate configuration change — it never auto-promotes itself.

What Gets Recorded

Every evaluation is logged with two critical timestamps:

  • bar_close_ts — which 15-minute bar the decision was based on (the data)
  • decision_ts — when MIP actually made the decision (the action)

This separation is essential. It proves MIP never uses future information in its decisions. Beyond timestamps, each log entry records: target return, current return, peak return (MFE), giveback percentage, gate pass/fail results, fee-adjusted P&L, and the delta versus holding to the planned horizon.

It piggybacks on the same hourly pipeline that already ingests intraday data. If intraday bars are missing for any symbol, that position is silently skipped — no crashes, no errors, no disruption.

Cost Awareness

The Decision Console polls Snowflake every 30 minutes (not continuously) to keep data warehouse costs minimal. Since the intraday pipeline runs hourly, this polling frequency ensures you see fresh data within one cycle while keeping the warehouse idle most of the time.

Scenario: A Typical Early-Exit Day

MIP buys AMZN at 9:35 AM based on a strong momentum signal, targeting +0.45% over 5 bars.

1 10:15 AM: AMZN hits +0.52% — target reached. Position becomes a Candidate (yellow badge in the console).

2 11:00 AM: AMZN pushes to +0.71% (MFE). Momentum is strong — no exit, let it run.

3 12:30 PM: AMZN pulls back to +0.38% — giveback is 46% from peak. Last 3 bars made no new high. Stage B triggers. Exit signal fires (red badge).

4 In Shadow mode: the exit is logged but the position stays open. The Decision Diff shows you would have locked in +0.38% versus the hold-to-horizon outcome. After 2–4 weeks of evidence, you can switch to Paper mode.

Appendix B

Frequently Asked Questions

Is MIP trading with real money?

No. MIP is a paper trading system. All money is virtual, all trades are simulated, and no real financial transactions occur. MIP has no connection to any broker or exchange.

Can MIP lose my money?

No. There is nothing real to lose. A portfolio going "bust" means its virtual value dropped below a threshold — a learning experience, not a financial loss.

How accurate are the signals?

It depends on the pattern, the asset, and market conditions. A pattern with a 60% hit rate is "right" about 60% of the time. MIP's trust system ensures only patterns with demonstrated accuracy (55%+) are used for trading decisions.

What happens when a pattern stops working?

MIP catches it automatically. During training, if a pattern's hit rate drops below 55% or its cumulative return turns negative, MIP deactivates it. No more signals from that pattern until performance improves.

Why does MIP need 40+ signals before trusting a pattern?

Statistical significance. If you flip a coin 5 times and get heads 4 times (80%), you wouldn't conclude the coin is rigged — that could easily be luck. But 550 heads out of 1,000 flips (55%) is much more convincing. MIP's threshold ensures decisions are based on evidence, not coincidence.

What is the difference between a signal and a trade?

A signal is a detection: "This asset meets the criteria." A trade is an action: "Buy this asset." Signals must earn trust, survive capacity limits, and pass risk checks before becoming trades. Many signals never become trades.

What does "drawdown" really mean in practical terms?

Imagine your portfolio grew from $100K to $120K, then dropped to $108K. Your drawdown is 10% — measured from the peak ($120K), not the start ($100K). You're still up $8K overall, but drawdown captures the pain of watching $12K in gains shrink to $8K.

Can different portfolios trade on the same signal?

Yes. Each portfolio applies its own risk rules independently. The same AAPL signal might trigger a trade in one portfolio but not another (because the second is full, or its risk gate is blocking).

How does MIP handle weekends and holidays?

Markets are closed on weekends and holidays, so no new bars exist. MIP carries forward the last available closing price when needed. The pipeline only processes days where market data actually exists.

Can I create my own patterns?

Yes. Patterns are configured through definitions with adjustable parameters (lookback windows, minimum returns, z-score thresholds). Add a new definition, and MIP will automatically start generating signals, evaluating outcomes, and building a track record for it.

What's the difference between the simulation and the proposal system?

The simulation replays the entire portfolio history from start to finish. The proposal system operates on today only: it looks at today's signals, suggests trades, validates them, and executes. Simulation = big picture replay. Proposals = "what should we do right now?"

Will the early-exit layer automatically close my positions?

Not by default. The system starts in Shadow mode, which logs what would happen but changes nothing. You must deliberately switch to Paper or Active mode after reviewing 2–4 weeks of shadow evidence.

Does the Decision Console use a lot of Snowflake credits?

No. The console polls every 30 minutes, which means only about 16 small queries per 8-hour market day. Since the intraday pipeline itself runs hourly, polling more often would not yield fresher data.

Can I see why MIP decided to exit (or not exit) a position?

Yes. Open the Position Inspector in the Decision Console and expand the Gate Trace. Each evaluation shows pass/fail for every check (payoff reached, giveback %, no-new-high count), with timestamps and raw JSON available for full audit.
